EXPEDITION 001 · APR 2026 · EUROPE

Ghosts, Gates
& Borrowed Trams

"Berlin doesn't greet you. It waits for you to figure it out. And when you do — it's unlike anywhere else on earth."

The City That Makes You Work For It

I arrived in Berlin at 11 PM knowing exactly one thing — my hostel was on Schwedter Straße in Prenzlauer Berg. Everything else was a mystery.

No map memorized. No German phrases prepared. No idea which side of the tram platform to stand on.

By the time I reached East Seven Hostel — dragging my bag through quiet cobblestone streets — Berlin had already made its first impression. Cool. Unbothered. Waiting.

I would spend the next three days trying to understand this city. Berlin, I quickly learned, rewards patience and punishes expectations.

✦ DAY ONE ✦

Where History Hits Like a Freight Train

There's a moment standing beneath Brandenburg Gate that no photograph captures.

It's not the scale — though the scale is staggering. It's the weight. The invisible, almost physical weight of everything this gate has witnessed. Division. War. Reunification. Hope. Standing in Pariser Platz looking up at those twelve Doric columns, I felt genuinely small in the best possible way.

I walked through slowly. Deliberately. Thinking about all the people who couldn't.

Five minutes south, the Holocaust Memorial stopped me completely.

2,711 concrete stelae. Varying heights. Uneven ground deliberately designed to disorient. No explanatory plaques among the blocks. No audio guide telling you how to feel. Just you, the grey columns, and a silence that grows louder the deeper you walk.

I went underground to the information center and stayed far longer than planned. Some places demand that.

Bebelplatz: The Empty Library

Unter den Linden came next — Berlin's grand baroque boulevard stretching east from the Gate toward Museum Island. I walked it slowly, stopping at Bebelplatz, where I crouched to peer through a small glass window set into the cobblestones.

Below — an underground room of empty white bookshelves.

The Empty Library. A memorial to the Nazi book burnings of May 1933. Somewhere between 20,000 and 25,000 books burned in that square. The empty shelves hold the memory of every one of them.

One of the most quietly devastating things I've ever seen. Free to visit. Impossible to forget.

Museum Island & an Evening in Mitte

By the time Museum Island appeared ahead — the magnificent green dome of Berliner Dom rising above the Spree, the reconstructed Humboldt Forum gleaming beside it — I felt like I'd lived three days in one morning. I sat by the river and just breathed.

That evening I found myself in the art nouveau courtyards of Hackesche Höfe — eight interconnected courtyards of ornate early-20th-century architecture, now buzzing with bars, galleries, and restaurants. Fairy lights strung between buildings. Laughter echoing off tiled walls.

Berlin by night is a completely different city. Warmer. More alive. More itself.

I took the Tram M1 back to my hostel feeling like a local. Small victory. Mine entirely.

✦ DAY TWO ✦

The Wall, The Weight & The Rain

If Day 1 was about grand boulevards and memorial silences, Day 2 was about the scar.

The East Side Gallery stretches 1.3 km along the Spree — the longest remaining section of the Berlin Wall, now the world's largest open-air gallery. 105 murals painted by artists from 21 countries after the Wall fell in 1989.

I walked its entire length slowly. The famous Brotherly Kiss — Brezhnev and Honecker locked in a surreal painted embrace — stopped me longest. Art and politics and history and dark humor compressed into one image on a concrete slab that once divided a city. Only in Berlin.

At Oberbaumbrücke, I stopped and turned back to look. One of the city's most beautiful bridges — red brick, twin towers, Gothic arches — connecting what was once East and West Berlin across the Spree. The wall murals stretching in one direction. The modern city in every other. One of those travel moments you try to photograph, but know the photo will never do justice.

Checkpoint Charlie was next — touristy, yes, absolutely crowded — but standing at the exact spot where Cold War tensions once brought the world to the edge of conflict, where people risked everything to cross a painted line on asphalt, the chills were real regardless of the souvenir shops.

Topography of Terror

Built on the former headquarters of the Gestapo and SS, this free museum is one of the most important places I've ever visited anywhere in the world. I took the full audio tour. I read every panel on every outdoor wall. I stood beside the preserved section of the Berlin Wall that runs alongside the building and tried to comprehend what happened in the ground beneath my feet.

Then it started raining. Heavy, relentless, Berlin rain.

I stayed inside even longer. Reading things that felt necessary to read. Some education is uncomfortable. The most important kind usually is.

When I finally emerged, the rain had softened to a drizzle and I wandered to Potsdamer Platz — a place that was literally death-strip wasteland 35 years ago, now a gleaming district of glass towers and corporate headquarters.

I found the Sony Center after some confused navigation I won't fully detail here. Standing beneath its spectacular glass dome, I looked down at the ground — a double line of cobblestones running across the plaza, marking exactly where the Berlin Wall once stood. Tourists walking over it without noticing. History, literally underfoot.

That evening I tried Berliner Weisse — Berlin's uniquely sour wheat beer, served with a shot of raspberry syrup. Strange, refreshing, and completely unlike anything else. Like Berlin itself.

✦ DAY THREE · POTSDAM ✦

Frederick the Great & The Dutch Quarter

My final Berlin day started with souvenirs. The Ampelmann Shop at Hackescher Markt — dedicated entirely to East Germany's beloved traffic light man, the little hat-wearing figure that Berliners loved so much they fought to keep him after reunification. I picked up a tote bag and some keychains. Lightweight. Distinctly Berlin.

Then the DDR Museum — and if you visit Berlin and skip this, you're making a mistake.

Interactive, irreverent, and occasionally heartbreaking, the DDR Museum reconstructs everyday East German life in a way no textbook can. I sat in an actual Trabant car. I rummaged through the drawers of a fully furnished East German apartment — toothbrushes, ration books, propaganda posters. I drove a Trabant simulation through grey 1980s East Berlin streets. Fascinating. Uncomfortable. Completely unmissable.

Sanssouci: Frederick the Great's Garden

Then the S7 to Potsdam. Sanssouci Palace sits atop terraced vineyards on the edge of the city, and it is — quite simply — one of the most beautiful things I've seen. Built by Frederick the Great between 1745 and 1747 — "Sanssouci" meaning "without worries" in French — it was his personal paradise away from the pressures of ruling Prussia.

I stood at his grave on the terrace. A simple flat stone. His name. Some flowers left by visitors. No grand monument. No royal fanfare. Just a king who built one of Europe's most beautiful palaces and asked to be buried in it quietly. There's something beautiful about that.

The Dutch Quarter followed — four improbable blocks of red-brick, Dutch-style houses built in the 18th century to attract craftsmen to Potsdam. Now full of independent cafés and boutiques, and photogenic from every angle.

I came home with a genuine piece of the Berlin Wall with certificate of authenticity. One piece of a divided world to carry home.

✦ FIELD NOTES ✦

What Berlin Taught Me

Berlin is not a comfortable city. It doesn't want to be.

It forces you to look at history directly — the beautiful parts and the horrific parts equally. It carries its scars proudly, turns them into art, builds memorials in the middle of its busiest streets, and dares you to walk past without stopping.

By the end of three days, I had navigated trams, U-Bahns, and S-Bahns. I had asked strangers for help and received it every single time. I had stood in places that changed the world, and walked streets that once couldn't be crossed.

"Berlin doesn't offer itself easily. But once it does — it stays with you forever."
