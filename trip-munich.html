EXPEDITION 002 · FEB 2026 · EUROPE

Munich
& the Castles

ICE 1507, Southbound

The ICE 1507 from Berlin to Munich covers 585 kilometres in under four hours. Germany moves past the window like a film reel: the flat northern plains giving way to rolling green hills, then the first distant suggestion of mountains on the horizon. I had a window seat, a coffee that cost €3.50, and no plans beyond the name of my hotel. Bavaria began to feel real somewhere around Nuremberg.

Munich Hauptbahnhof is vast and purposeful — the kind of station that makes you feel that something significant is about to happen. I came out onto Bahnhofplatz in weak winter sunshine, dragged my bag to the U-Bahn, and let the city start.

✦ DAY ONE ✦

Marienplatz: The Heart of Bavaria

Marienplatz is Munich's central square, dominated by the neo-Gothic Neues Rathaus with its famous Glockenspiel — 43 bells and 32 figures that perform twice daily at eleven and noon. I arrived at eleven, stood with several hundred other people in the square, craned my neck upward, and watched the figures rotate and joust in the cold air. It lasts about fifteen minutes. The crowd was rapt. There is something about watching an eight-hundred-year-old tradition performed by medieval mechanical figures that suspends all cynicism.

The Frauenkirche — Our Lady's Cathedral — stands just off the square, its twin onion-domed towers visible from much of the city. Inside it is spare and enormous: bare brick, high arches, pale light through plain windows. Near the entrance is the Devil's Footprint: a single dark impression in the stone floor, said to be where the devil stamped his foot when he realised the church's architect had tricked him. Whether you believe the story or not, the footprint is real. I stood on it briefly. Nothing happened. I consider this a win.

Hofbräuhaus: One Litre of Bavaria

You cannot go to Munich and avoid Hofbräuhaus. It would be like going to Paris and skipping the Eiffel Tower — technically possible, but missing the point entirely. I went on the first evening. The hall seats around a thousand people and was, at 7pm on a Tuesday in February, more than half full. Long wooden tables. Dirndl-wearing waitresses carrying three one-litre steins in each hand. An oompah band in the corner, red-faced and committed, playing something that made everyone nod along whether they meant to or not.

I ordered a Maß of Hacker-Pschorr — the original house beer — and a Schweinshaxe: a roasted pork knuckle the approximate size of a human head, served with potato dumplings and dark gravy. I ate alone at the end of a long table, surrounded by strangers from four different countries who were all, for the duration of that meal, exactly as happy as people can be. The oompah band played Ein Prosit four times. Each time, everyone raised their glass on cue. I raised mine too. There are traditions you adopt immediately.

✦ DAY TWO ✦

Oberammergau & the Road to Linderhof

On the second day I took a tour bus south into the Bavarian Alps. The first stop was Oberammergau — a small village famous for its Passion Play (performed every ten years since 1634) and its remarkable tradition of façade painting: entire building fronts covered in trompe-l'oeil scenes of Biblical stories, pastoral landscapes, and elaborate folk designs. The effect, walking down a village street in February with snow still on the ground, is hallucinatory. Nothing looks quite solid.

From Oberammergau, the bus climbed higher into the mountains to Linderhof — the smallest of King Ludwig II's three palaces and the only one he lived in long enough to actually enjoy. It sits in a formal French garden in a valley ringed by forest: terraced fountains, manicured hedges, a small temple on the hillside. Inside, the rooms are relentlessly gilded — gold on the walls, gold on the ceiling, gold on the furniture. The dining table could be lowered through the floor into the kitchen below, allowing Ludwig to eat alone without ever encountering his servants. The man had a complicated relationship with other people.

Below the palace, in the hillside, is the Venus Grotto: an artificial stalactite cave built around a small lake, illuminated by coloured lights, containing a golden shell-shaped boat in which Ludwig would float in the dark and listen to Wagner performed by musicians hidden on the far shore. I have no way to improve on this fact. It is already perfect.

✦ NEUSCHWANSTEIN ✦

The Castle in the Clouds

Neuschwanstein is where the road ends and the fairy tale begins. It sits on a rocky outcrop above the village of Hohenschwangau, half-hidden by forest and cloud, its white limestone towers rising against whatever sky the Alps have decided to provide that day. Ludwig II began building it in 1869 and never finished it. He died in 1886 — found floating face-down in the Starnberger See under circumstances that have never been fully explained — and construction stopped with 15 rooms complete out of a planned 200.

The completed rooms are extraordinary. The Throne Room has no throne — Ludwig died before it was installed — but the space designed to hold it is Byzantine and vast: cobalt blue ceiling studded with gilt stars, a two-storey mosaic floor of animals and plants, columns of red porphyry. A throne was planned here that was never placed. The absence of it is somehow more powerful than any throne could have been.

The Singer's Hall was built for concerts that were never held. Ludwig's bedroom took four and a half years to carve — every surface covered with scenes from Tristan and Isolde — and he slept in it for eleven nights. The guide told us this with a tone that suggested she had made peace with the extravagance. I had not.

A Conversation at Altitude

After the tour, waiting for the bus back, I fell into conversation with two people: a 73-year-old retired schoolteacher from California who had always wanted to see Neuschwanstein and had finally come alone after her husband died the previous year, and a young Japanese couple on their honeymoon who had driven from Salzburg that morning specifically for this.

We stood together at the viewpoint above the Marienbrücke — the bridge that gives you the famous view of the castle — while the mountains turned pink in the late afternoon light, and talked about why people travel. The Californian said she thought it was about proving something to yourself. The Japanese woman said she thought it was about collecting memories to give to people you love. Her husband nodded and said nothing, which was its own kind of answer.

The bus came. We all got on it and went our separate ways. I don't know any of their names. I remember every word of the conversation.
